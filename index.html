AI FAQ for Teachers

Your practical guide to using AI tools in education

Getting Started

Start small with something familiar and low-stakes. Try having AI help you write a parent email you've written many times before, or create a simple worksheet for a topic you know well. Compare the AI output to what you would have created yourself - this helps you understand how the AI thinks and produces content.

If you have access to NSW EduChat, start there as it's designed for education. Otherwise, ChatGPT is user-friendly and versatile. Create a free account and experiment with simple tasks like "Help me write a friendly reminder email to parents about library books."

This is exactly why you start with low-stakes tasks! Try AI for something you could easily do yourself, so you can spot problems and learn what works. Never use AI for anything critical until you're confident in how it behaves.

Effective Usage

Be specific and provide context. Instead of "Create a lesson plan," try "Create a 45-minute Year 3 science lesson about plant life cycles, including a hands-on activity for 25 students with limited materials." The more details you provide, the better the output.

Include these details:

  • Year level and subject
  • Learning objectives or curriculum standards
  • Time available
  • Class size and any special considerations
  • Available resources
  • Desired activity types

Ask AI to create multiple versions of the same content at different levels. For example: "Create this comprehension passage at three reading levels: below grade level, at grade level, and above grade level for Year 4 students."

Administrative Tasks

AI is excellent for drafting routine communications. Give it the key points and ask for appropriate tone. For example: "Write a polite email to parents explaining that their child needs to complete missing homework. Keep it supportive and solution-focused."

AI can quickly generate content, but always review for accuracy and appropriateness. Try: "Create 10 word problems for Year 5 multiplication using real-world scenarios that would interest 10-11 year olds."

AI can help generate comment bank phrases and suggest ways to phrase observations constructively. But remember - the insights about individual students must come from you.

Quality and Ethics

Always fact-check important information, especially in subjects like science, history, or current events. Use AI as a starting point, not the final word. Cross-reference with reliable sources.

Consider your school's policy, but generally transparency builds trust. You might say "I used AI to help create the first draft of this, then adapted it specifically for our class."

Avoid AI for:

  • Final grades or high-stakes assessments without review
  • Sensitive communications with parents about serious issues
  • Content about controversial topics without fact-checking
  • Anything that replaces your professional judgment about individual students

Tips and Tricks

Getting Better Prompts:

  • Be specific about format: "Create a table with three columns" or "Write this as bullet points"
  • Specify audience: "Write this for parents" vs "Write this for Year 3 students"
  • Ask for examples: "Include three specific examples"
  • Request multiple options: "Give me three different approaches"

When asking AI to create structured content, mention markdown formatting:

  • "Create a lesson plan using markdown headings and bullet points"
  • "Format this as a table using markdown"
  • "Use markdown to make the important parts bold"

This makes the output more organized and easier to copy into other documents.

Iterative Prompting: Don't expect perfection on the first try:

  1. Start with a basic request
  2. Ask for modifications: "Make this more engaging for reluctant readers"
  3. Request additions: "Add an extension activity for fast finishers"
  4. Refine tone: "Make the language more formal/casual/encouraging"

Time-Saving Phrases:

  • "In the style of..." (Roald Dahl, a newspaper article, etc.)
  • "Appropriate for..." (struggling readers, gifted students, EAL learners)
  • "Include scaffolding for..."
  • "Make this more engaging by..."
  • "Simplify this without losing the key concepts"

Troubleshooting

Add more specific context and constraints. Instead of asking for "a science experiment," try "a 15-minute chemistry demonstration for Year 8 using only materials found in a typical classroom."

Be explicit about the level: "Explain photosynthesis for Year 4 students who are just learning about plants" or "Challenge Year 10 students who already understand basic chemistry."
